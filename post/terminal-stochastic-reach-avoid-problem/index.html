Safety for stochastic systems: Terminal stochastic reach-avoid problem

Last updated on May 9, 2018 4 min read

Automation, typically, refers to the use of an onboard computer to drives a system to optimize some objective. It makes our day-to-day life simpler and efficient, for example, in transportation (autopilot systems and self-driving cars), space applications (spacecraft rendezvous and docking problem), biomedical applications, power grid, and the list goes on and on. However, as we hand over more and more control to the automation, we naturally seek guarantees of safety and performance. After all, we do not want to spend billions of dollar in R&D and develop an automation that may cause harm.

Why is providing safety guarantees in automated control systems for real-world problems hard?

In control theory, we attack real-world problems by formulating a mathematical model and studying its relevant properties. The two key challenges in providing the desired safety guarantees for real-world systems are stochasticity and high-dimensionality.

Stochasticity in our models help us capture human inputs (like actions of the other drivers on the street for the self-driving car problem), disturbance effects (like weather in air navigation/power grid systems), varying parameters (like patient parameters in biomedical devices), and other unmodelled phenomena (like simplifications1 done in complex system models seen in space and power applications). High-dimensional systems evolve as a function of a larger set of states, and thereby allowing the mathematical model to be closer to the reality compared to their low-dimensional models.

We pose the problem of safety as Problem A is: Design a controller that maximizes the probability of satisfaction of the following constraints under bounded control space for the system:

  1. Ensure that the system reaches the target set at a finite time horizon (not before). (Reach constraint)
  2. Ensure that the system stays within the safe set at a finite time horizon (not before). (Reach constraint)

Problem A is accompanied by the following information:

  1. the initial state of the system,
  2. the safe set,
  3. the target set,
  4. the stochastic system dynamics along with the bounded control space.

Problem A is called the terminal hitting-time stochastic reach-avoid problem1. It is a special class of verification problems, which has been well studied for the past couple of decades in computer science as well as control theory.

Safety verification explained via an example: the spacecraft rendezvous and docking problem

Consider the spacecraft rendezvous and docking problem. As shown in Figure 2, we have two satellites — a “chief” satellite and a “deputy” satellite. To simplify the study, we consider the dynamics of the deputy satellites relative to the chief satellite, and in contrast to a true mathematical model, we consider a simpler stochastic model to describe the relative dynamics.

Our safety and performance requirements are:

  1. Ensure that the deputy satellite reaches the chief satellite at a finite time horizon (not before). (Reach constraint)
  2. Ensure that the deputy satellite remains in a line-of-sight cone till it satisfies objective 1. (Avoid constraint)

We are interested in solving Problem A: Design a controller that maximizes the probability of satisfaction of the above objectives 1 and 2 under bounded control space for the deputy satellite.

In this case, we have the following information:

  1. the initial state of the deputy satellite,
  2. the safe set (definition of the line-of-sight cone),
  3. the target set (a box around the origin),
  4. the relative stochastic dynamics of the deputy satellite with respect to the chief satellite.

Note that the avoid constraint is motivated by the fact that sensing and localization capabilities are limited in space applications.
